Men's Mental Health

Men experience mental health challenges differently, and they tend to seek help later, if at all. This guide covers the most common issues men face, how they tend to show up, and what therapy can actually do about them.

Men in Ontario are significantly less likely than women to seek mental health support. They are also three times more likely to die by suicide. These two facts are not unrelated.

The barriers are real. Some are cultural, the expectation that men should handle things themselves, that asking for help is weakness, that talking about feelings is uncomfortable or unnecessary. Some are practical, not knowing what therapy actually involves or whether it would even help. Some are about recognition: many men do not identify what they are experiencing as depression, anxiety, or grief. It just feels like being tired, irritable, disconnected, or flat.

What tends to change when men do reach out is that the problems that felt unnameable start to have names. And named problems are much easier to work with.

How mental health looks different in men

The classic picture of depression, persistent sadness, crying, hopelessness, is less common in men than the clinical literature would suggest. Men with depression more often present with irritability, short tempers, risk-taking, overworking, withdrawal from relationships, and increased substance use. It is easy to mistake for personality, stress, or just "being a guy."

The same is true for anxiety. Men are less likely to describe worry or fear. They are more likely to describe restlessness, difficulty concentrating, tension, or an inability to wind down. They reach for alcohol, work, gaming, or pornography as ways of managing the pressure, often without recognizing that the pressure is anxiety.

This does not mean men suffer more than women. It means they often suffer differently, and in ways that are harder to see from the outside.

What therapy looks like for men

Many men come to therapy not knowing what to expect, and half-expecting it to feel like a lecture or a confessional. It is neither. Sessions are practical and collaborative. You bring what is going on. Matt listens, asks useful questions, and helps you understand your patterns more clearly. There is no pressure to perform emotion you do not feel.

For men who are sceptical, a free 20-minute consultation is a no-risk way to find out whether working together makes sense.
