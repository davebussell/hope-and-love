Performance Anxiety Therapy

Performance anxiety is the fear of failing in situations where something feels at stake. At work, in relationships, sexually, socially. The anxiety is real, and the avoidance it creates often makes things worse. Therapy addresses the cycle, not just the symptoms.

Most people experience nerves before something that matters. Performance anxiety is something different: a pattern of fear and avoidance that persists, intensifies, and starts to limit what you are willing to attempt. It is not just pre-game nerves. It is the voice that says you are going to fail before you even begin, and the strategies you build to avoid finding out if it is right.

Types of performance anxiety

Performance anxiety shows up in several different contexts, and the same person often experiences more than one.

Work and professional performance anxiety

Fear of being exposed as incompetent. Difficulty speaking up in meetings. Procrastination driven by fear of producing imperfect work. Avoiding promotion opportunities because the visibility feels threatening. Perfectionism that leads to paralysis rather than excellence.

For many people this is connected to impostor syndrome, the persistent sense that they have only gotten as far as they have through luck or deception, and that they are one mistake away from being found out.

Sexual performance anxiety

Anxiety about sexual performance is extremely common in men and rarely talked about openly. It can present as difficulty maintaining arousal, premature ejaculation, avoidance of intimacy, or a pattern of anxiety that has become so associated with sex that it activates before anything even starts.

Sexual performance anxiety is often worsened by pornography use, which sets unrealistic expectations and creates a pattern of solo sexual experience that does not translate to partnered intimacy. It can also follow a specific negative experience that has taken on disproportionate meaning.

The shame around sexual performance anxiety tends to be significant, and it keeps people from addressing it. Most men who seek support for it find that it is much more treatable than they assumed.

Social performance anxiety

Fear of being judged, of saying something awkward, of not knowing how to read a room. Social performance anxiety overlaps with social anxiety but tends to be more situation-specific: presentations, networking events, first dates, group settings with people you want to impress.

The anxiety and avoidance cycle

What makes performance anxiety sticky is the way it self-reinforces. The anxiety leads to avoidance. The avoidance prevents the corrective experience that would reduce the anxiety. And the absence of that experience allows the anxious prediction to stay intact, unchallenged.

For sexual performance anxiety specifically, avoidance of intimacy protects against the feared outcome in the short term but damages the relationship and deepens the shame over time. For work anxiety, procrastination and over-preparation create their own costs.

How performance anxiety connects to shame and addiction

Performance anxiety is almost always tangled with shame. The fear of failure is really the fear of being revealed as inadequate. And shame about that inadequacy leads to concealment, which leads to more isolation, and often to using substances or compulsive behaviour as a way of managing the weight of it.

Understanding these connections is part of what therapy does. The anxiety is rarely just about the performance.

What therapy covers

Treatment is tailored to your specific presentation. It typically involves identifying the beliefs driving the anxiety, understanding the avoidance patterns that have formed, and gradually building a different relationship with the situations that trigger you. CBT and ACT approaches are commonly useful. The work on shame and self-worth runs alongside the practical tools.

Sessions are 50 minutes, available in-person in Ontario or via secure video province-wide.

Common Questions

Frequently asked questions

Is sexual performance anxiety something therapy can actually help with?
Yes, consistently. Sexual performance anxiety responds well to therapy, particularly when there is no underlying medical cause. The anxiety is real, but it is also a learned pattern, and patterns can be changed. Many men find it more addressable than they expected once they talk about it honestly.
Do I need to talk about sex in detail?
You control the level of detail. Matt works from what you bring, at your pace. Some people find it useful to be very specific; others work at a higher level. Either is fine. The goal is to understand your pattern, not to recreate the experience in the room.
I've had this for years. Is it too late to change?
No. Performance anxiety can be addressed at any point. Longer-standing patterns may take more time to shift, but duration is not a barrier to change. The fact that you have managed it for years also means you understand it better than you think.
Can work performance anxiety be treated separately from other issues?
Often. Work performance anxiety can be addressed on its own if that is the presenting concern. If there are connections to other areas of anxiety, depression, or substance use, those may come up naturally and can be addressed as part of the same work or separately, depending on what makes sense for you.
