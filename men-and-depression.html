Depression · Men's Mental Health

Men and Depression

Depression in men rarely looks like the textbook version. It tends to show up as irritability, withdrawal, numbness, or exhaustion rather than visible sadness, which is one reason it goes unrecognized for so long.

Men are diagnosed with depression at roughly half the rate of women. They are also three times more likely to die by suicide. The gap between those two numbers tells you something important: depression in men is not less common, it is less visible. Often to the people around them, and often to themselves.

Part of the difficulty is that depression does not always look the way most people expect. Many men with depression do not describe feeling sad. They describe feeling flat. Or exhausted in a way that sleep does not fix. Or irritable over things that would not have bothered them before. Or just checked out, going through the motions without any real feeling behind it.

That combination of symptoms is easy to explain away. Work stress. Getting older. Not sleeping enough. And so months or years can pass before the pattern gets named for what it is.

How depression tends to present in men

Male depression does not follow a single script, but some patterns come up often enough to be worth naming.

  • Irritability and low frustration tolerance. Snapping at small things. A shorter fuse than usual. Reacting to situations that would not have registered before.
  • Emotional numbness. Not sadness exactly, but a flatness. Not finding enjoyment in things that used to matter. Going through the motions at home, at work, in relationships.
  • Withdrawal. Pulling back from friends, family, activities. Not dramatic disappearance, just gradual disengagement. Saying no more often. Spending more time alone.
  • Overworking. Using work or busyness as a way to avoid sitting with how things actually feel. Productivity as avoidance.
  • Increased substance use. Drinking more than usual. Using alcohol, cannabis, or other substances to take the edge off at the end of the day, and finding the amount creeping up.
  • Physical symptoms. Fatigue, sleep disruption, changes in appetite, and unexplained physical pain are all common with depression and more likely to be the presenting concern in men.
  • Risk-taking. Some men with depression become more reckless, driving faster, making impulsive decisions, engaging in behaviour that feels like not caring what happens.

Why men do not seek help sooner

There are several reasons, and they layer on each other.

One is recognition. If you do not recognize what you are experiencing as depression, you are not going to look for depression treatment. Many men attribute their symptoms to external circumstances: a bad season at work, relationship tension, getting older.

Another is the way men are socialized to handle difficulty. Pushing through. Not making a fuss. Handling things internally. These are not bad instincts in every situation, but they become a liability when the thing that needs attention is your own mental health.

There is also a genuine uncertainty about what therapy involves. Many men have never been. They do not know whether it will feel useful or feel like a performance. That uncertainty is worth naming, because it is a reasonable concern, not a character flaw.

What untreated depression costs

Depression that is not addressed tends to get worse before it gets better on its own. And the costs accumulate. Relationships strain under the weight of withdrawal and irritability. Work performance suffers. Substance use increases. The gap between who a man knows himself to be and how he is actually showing up widens, and that gap itself becomes a source of shame.

None of that is inevitable. Depression responds well to treatment, often including therapy, and sometimes in combination with medical support.

What therapy for depression looks like

Sessions are 50 minutes, available in-person in Ontario or via secure video anywhere in the province. The first priority is understanding what is actually happening, not fitting you into a framework or working from a checklist. Matt draws on evidence-based approaches including CBT and Motivational Interviewing, adapted to what is actually useful for you.

You do not have to be in crisis to come to therapy. You do not have to be certain that you are depressed. Feeling like something is off and not knowing how to talk about it is a completely valid reason to reach out.

Common Questions

Frequently asked questions

How do I know if what I'm experiencing is depression or just stress?
The distinction matters less than whether what you are experiencing is affecting your life. Depression and chronic stress can look very similar, and both are worth addressing in therapy. You do not need a diagnosis before you reach out. If you feel like something is off and it has been that way for a while, that is enough of a reason to talk.
Is therapy useful for men, or is it mostly designed for women?
Therapy works for men. The approach matters, and Matt works in a way that is direct, practical, and focused on understanding patterns rather than just processing feelings for their own sake. Many men find it more useful than they expected once they try it.
Do I need to talk about my childhood or my emotions to make progress?
Not necessarily. Some of the most useful work in therapy is practical: understanding what triggers a response, recognizing patterns, developing different ways of handling things. Past experience comes up when it is relevant. You are not required to go there before you are ready.
What if I've tried to feel better on my own and it hasn't worked?
That is actually one of the clearest signs that something more structured would help. Depression has a way of making the strategies that normally work feel unavailable or ineffective. That is not a failure of willpower. It is how the condition works, and it is exactly the kind of thing therapy is built to address.
