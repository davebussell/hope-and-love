Find Support for My Relationship
For Couples
Addiction doesn't just affect one person. It reshapes the relationship, the trust, the intimacy, the communication, the future you thought you were building together.

When addiction enters a relationship, it changes things. The trust shifts. The intimacy fades. Conversations that used to come easily start to feel impossible, and both people can end up feeling alone even when they are in the same room.

For the partner, it can feel like loving someone has quietly become one of the most exhausting things in your life. And for the person struggling, the weight of what their addiction has cost the relationship can make it even harder to ask for help.

Couples therapy is not about deciding who is to blame.

It is about creating enough safety and honesty that both people can actually talk, and actually be heard. From there, couples can start to rebuild trust, set healthier expectations, and figure out what kind of future they want to work toward.

Some couples come in on the edge of separation. Others come in early, wanting to get ahead of the damage. Both are the right time to reach out.

Registered Psychotherapist • Ontario

Matt brings warmth, honesty, and clinical expertise to every session. His work is grounded in a conviction that healing requires honesty, support, and safety, and that every person and every couple has the capacity for genuine change.

How We Work

Couples therapy that meets you where you are

Step 01

Free 20-minute consultation

No paperwork, no commitment. We get to know each other and make sure working together is the right fit before anything else.

Step 02

Shared goals, honest conversation

Together we clarify what each partner needs, what the relationship needs, and what healing can realistically look like from here.

Step 03

Ongoing sessions, practical progress

Weekly or bi-weekly sessions, in-person or secure video, with consistent support, honest accountability, and compassion for both partners.

Love is not denial, rescuing, enabling, or staying in unsafe situations. Healthy love includes boundaries, wisdom, and the courage to ask for support. Boundaries can be an act of love.

Matt Harris, RP

A note on safety: Hope does not mean ignoring harm, minimizing betrayal, or staying in unsafe situations. Relationship repair is only considered where safety, willingness, and support are present. Not every relationship can or should be restored, and we will never pressure you toward an outcome that is not right for you.
